# Phase 1: Project Definition

## 1. Problem Statement
The global trajectory of climate change and economic development presents an unprecedented challenge. Between 2020 and 2050, the world must balance rapid population growth and the need for economic expansion (especially in developing nations) with the urgent necessity of drastically reducing carbon emissions and transitioning to sustainable energy. Failure to understand and manage this nexus risks catastrophic environmental damage, economic instability, and widened global inequality.

## 2. Research Questions
1. What are the historical and projected relationships between GDP growth, energy consumption, and carbon emissions globally and regionally?
2. How will population growth (particularly in Africa and Asia) impact future energy demands?
3. Which countries or regions are successfully decoupling economic growth from carbon emissions, and what policies enable this?
4. What are the most likely global scenarios for temperature anomalies by 2050 based on current vs. accelerated renewable energy adoption?

## 3. Objectives
- To quantify the impact of demographic shifts on energy demand and emissions.
- To identify the major drivers of economic growth that do not proportionately increase carbon footprints.
- To forecast key global indicators (Population, GDP, Energy, Emissions, Temperature) to 2050 under three scenarios: Optimistic, Realistic, and Worst-Case.
- To provide actionable, data-driven recommendations for policymakers and business leaders.

## 4. Expected Outcomes
- A robust, validated machine learning model capable of predicting emissions and energy needs based on demographic and economic inputs.
- An interactive dashboard providing scenario analysis for stakeholders.
- A comprehensive report detailing regional disparities and actionable strategies.

## 5. Stakeholders
- **Governments & Policymakers**: Need data to set emission targets, draft regulations, and allocate infrastructure budgets.
- **Investors & Financial Institutions**: Need to identify risks related to stranded fossil fuel assets and opportunities in green tech.
- **International Organizations (UN, World Bank)**: Require macroeconomic forecasts to direct global aid and development programs.
- **Energy Sector Corporations**: Must forecast demand to transition their energy portfolios.

## 6. Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
- **Model Accuracy**: RMSE and MAPE of time-series forecasts.
- **Carbon Intensity of GDP**: (CO2 emissions / GDP) - tracking decoupling trends.
- **Renewable Energy Transition Rate**: Annual percentage increase in renewable share.
- **Per Capita Emissions**: Tracking equity between developed and developing nations.

## Why This Problem Matters Globally
Climate change is a threat multiplier. If energy consumption and economic development remain coupled with high carbon emissions, the resultant temperature anomalies will disrupt agriculture, health, and economies globally. Conversely, stifling economic growth to cut emissions unfairly penalizes developing nations. Finding the optimal data-backed pathway is the defining challenge of the 21st century.
